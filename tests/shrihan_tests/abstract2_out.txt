Using a sample of 151 galaxies with dynamically measured black hole (BH) masses (M sub BH), we investigate the scaling relations between M sub BH and the stellar velocity dispersion, sigma, and, for a subsample of 30 core-S\'ersic galaxies, between M sub BH and the size of the partially depleted core, R sub b. Core-S\'ersic galaxies, identified using high-resolution Hubble Space Telescope imaging and spanning both the normal-core (R sub b less than 0.5 kiloparsecs) and large-core (R sub b greater than 0.5 kiloparsecs) regimes, define an updated \mboxM sub BH--R sub b relation of the form M sub BH proportional to R sub b to the 1.16 plus or minus 0.10, with an rms scatter of delta sub rms approximately equal to 0.28 dex in \log M sub BH. We find that S\'ersic and normal-core galaxies together follow a common log-linear M sub BH--sigma relation with a slope of 4.95 plus or minus 0.29 and a scatter delta sub rms approximately equal to 0.46 dex. Deviations from this relation arise at the highest BH masses, where large-core galaxies, including six with direct M sub BH measurements, drive a significant upturn. We find that these galaxies typically host ultramassive black holes whose masses scale more strongly with R sub b than sigma, and lie approximately (1--4) times the intrinsic scatter (0.39 dex) above the relation defined by S\'ersic and normal-core galaxies. The M sub BH--R sub b relation shows approximately 30--47percent less scatter in \log M sub BH than the corresponding M sub BH--sigma relation for the same sample. We interpret the high-mass upturn in the M sub BH--sigma diagram as a consequence of successive major, dry mergers, a scenario that naturally explains the observed flattening of the sigma-L sub V relation at M sub V \la -23.5 magnitudes.
