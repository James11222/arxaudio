We investigate the molecular gas content and interstellar medium (ISM) conditions of REBELS-25, a massive, star-forming galaxy at z equals 7.31. Deep VLA Q-band and ALMA Band 3 observations reveal CO(3--2) and CO(7--6) emission (both at approximately 3.5sigma), and provide an upper limit on [C i](2--1). From the CMB-corrected CO(3--2) flux--representing the highest-redshift detection of a low-J CO transition to date--we derive a molecular gas mass of M sub mol=(1.0 plus or minus 0.4) times 10 to the 11 (alpha sub CO/(3 solar masses(K kilometers per second parsecs squared) to the power of -1)) solar masses, directly confirming the presence of a very massive gas reservoir only approximately equal to 700 megayears after the Big Bang. This implies an extreme gas fraction of f sub gas approximately equal to 0.95, a gas-to-dust ratio of delta sub GDR approximately equal to 6 times 10 to the 2, and a depletion timescale of tau sub dep approximately equal to 1.2 gigayears, broadly consistent with extrapolated scaling relations for main-sequence galaxies at lower redshift. Using the radiative transfer code TUNER, we self-consistently model CO and dust continuum emission in the context of the significant CMB background, constraining ISM properties and recovering M sub mol= (1.8 to the +1.0sub -0.9) times 10 to the 11 solar masses, independent of assumptions about r sub 31 and alpha sub CO. We further discuss the use of alternative molecular gas tracers at early epochs. Combining CO and [C ii] measurements, we infer an empirical [C ii]-to-H sub 2 conversion factor of alpha sub [C ii]=(60 plus or minus 25) solar masses/solar luminosities, suggesting [C ii] remains a viable molecular gas tracer in the Epoch of Reionization. These results demonstrate the detectability of low-J CO emission even at z greater than 7, paving the way for next-generation facilities, and provide critical insights into the rapid mass assembly of galaxies during the first billion years of cosmic history.
